Mattis-Bardeen vs TLS Separation

Why a heated membrane inductor with a colder substrate capacitor helps isolate thermal response.

Home Project Physics Theory Noise Sources MB vs TLS Design Parameters Performance & Risks

Design Objective

Separate membrane-temperature-driven resonator response from substrate TLS drift.

Target configuration:

  • Inductor thermally tied to a membrane that can heat during events/readout loading.
  • Capacitor/dielectric region thermally tied closer to bath temperature.

Physical Basis

TLS frequency drift follows dielectric defect temperature in the capacitor/substrate region.

Mattis-Bardeen (MB) electrodynamic response follows quasiparticle temperature in the superconducting film.

So if dielectric temperature is approximately fixed while film temperature on the membrane changes:

  • TLS contribution is comparatively stable.
  • Kinetic-inductance/MB contribution carries most of the membrane-temperature-dependent frequency shift.

Applicability at Low Temperature

For a \(T_c \sim 3\) K film at \(T \sim 30\) mK (\(T/T_c \sim 0.01\)), MB is still the correct baseline model for equilibrium superconducting electrodynamics.

Practical caveats that can mask equilibrium MB behavior:

  • Nonequilibrium quasiparticles from readout power or pair-breaking radiation.
  • Residual thermal coupling that warms capacitor dielectric regions.
  • Film-disorder effects requiring generalized MB treatment.

Measurement Strategy

To validate MB/TLS separation experimentally:

  • Measure \(f_r(T)\) and \(Q_i(T)\) under low-readout-power conditions.
  • Repeat with controlled membrane heating while monitoring bath/substrate stability.
  • Compare low-temperature drift against a TLS-like baseline from dielectric-dominated modes/geometries.
