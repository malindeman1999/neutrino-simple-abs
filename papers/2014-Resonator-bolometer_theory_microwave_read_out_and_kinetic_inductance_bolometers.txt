Resonator-bolometer theory, microwave read out, and kinetic
inductance bolometers
M. A. Lindeman
Jet Propulsion Laboratory, California Institute of Technology, Pasadena, California 91109, USA
(Received 27 March 2014; accepted 1 July 2014; published online 14 July 2014)
Kinetic inductance bolometers and calorimeters, each consisting of a kinetic inductance device
suspended on a membrane and embedded in a resonant circuit, are being developed for
applications such as planetary science, climate science, and X-ray spectroscopy. Arrays of these
resonator-bolometers, each with a unique resonance frequency, are coupled to a single feedline,
allowing many bolometers or calorimeters to be multiplexed using microwave read out. We derive
coupled linear differential equations describing resonator-bolometers and means for calculating
responses to signal and noise sources. By employing the bolometer matrix formalism, the model
compactly describes the effects of demodulation, detuning, electrothermal feedback, resonator to
feedline coupling, and bolometer sensitivity to changes in temperature and bias current. Based on
this theory, estimates for the bolometer response to phonon noise, Johnson noise, and microwave
bias quasiparticle generation noise are derived. The model is represented in terms of accessible
parameters, most of which are measurable using a network analyzer. It is applicable to other types
of devices such as dielectric bolometers or alternating current biased transition edge sensors and is
readily extendible to more complex bolometers or to unsuspended kinetic inductance devices.

I. INTRODUCTION
The microwave kinetic inductance detector 1–5(MKID)
concept has been rapidly developed over the past decade to
enable large arrays of MKIDs to be deployed at telescopes
around the world.
6 These devices employ microwave read-
out, which allows large numbers of devices to be readout on
a single feedline using standard microwave electronics. In
this scheme, each detector is embedded in a resonator with a
unique resonance frequency. Signals in a detector alter the
resonance frequency and the quality factor Q of the resona-
tor. A generator sends a comb of microwave frequencies to
bias each of the resonators at resonance, and the responses of
the resonators to signals in the detectors are read out. The
fabrication of these arrays is relatively simple because the
detector, resonator, and feedline are typically patterned from
a single layer of superconductor.
Recently, a new class of devices has been under devel-
opment in which the MKID is suspended on a membrane so
that it functions as a thermal bolometer or calorimeter.
Application of such bolometers includes mid and far infra-
red spectrometers for planetary science missions and earth
observing satellites.
7–9 Suspended MKIDs are also being
developed as sensitive x-ray detectors capable of measuring
the energies of individual photons. 10 In these bolometric
designs, the membrane is thermally decoupled from the bath
temperature by a weak thermal link so that heat thermalizes
on the membrane before it leaks out. In this mode, the bo-
lometer acts as a single body at temperature T.T h i sr e d u c e s
various forms of internal thermal ﬂuctuation noise

associated with the exchange of energy between compo-

generation-recombination noise. Short quasiparticle
lifetimes are preferable in these kinetic inductance bolome-
ters (KIBs) because short lifetimes improve thermalization
within the bolometer, unlike in standard MKIDs, for which
short quasiparticle lifetimes are associated with rapid loss of
energy out of the detector and a reduction of the signal to
noise ratio.
The model of alternating current (AC) bolometers in
Ref. 12 describes some of the physics of resonator bolome-
ters, but does not include the effects of coupling to a feed-
line for microwave readout nor is the model described in
terms that are directly applicable to feedline coupled
devices. Reference 7 presents a brief description of how to
modify the AC bolometer matrix to apply it to feedline
coupled devices to ﬁnd the signal and the phonon noise in a
KIB on resonance. However, no description of other impor-
tant noise sources in these devices is given, such as quasi-
particle generation noise, Johnson noise, and ampliﬁer
noise, nor have the effects of detuning on a KIB been
described.
In this paper, new results are presented to aid in the de-
velopment of KIBs. Accessible calculations of various sour-
ces of noise are included to predict performance of KIBs and
related calorimeters. A new calculation for quasiparticle gen-
eration noise is presented, which includes effects omitted in
previous estimates. The effects of detuning are included by
the use of a rotation matrix. Detuning is also found to result
in a new kind of electrothermal feedback (ETF), which can
alter the thermal response time of the bolometers.
Differential equations for the evolution of the phase and am-
plitude are derived, which enable computations of the stabil-
ity and dynamics of the bolometers. A method for computing
the temperature sensitivity of KIBs using superconductivity
theory is also described.

VI. BOLOMETER NOISE
Ideally, the dominant source of noise within the band-
width of a kinetic inductance bolometer is phonon noise
associated with exchange of energy across the weak thermal
link G. Electronic noise from the bolometer, which equals or
exceeds Johnson noise, limits the usable bandwidth of the

For positive detuning, a positive temperature ﬂuctuation
causes increased detuning, resulting in decreased power in
the bolometer. The decreased power dissipation cools the

cooling alone. This negative feedback broadens the thermal
bandwidth, reduces the gain within the thermal bandwidth,
and decreases the electronic noise within the thermal band-
width, in a similar fashion to electrothermal feedback in
other types of bolometers.

VIII. DISCUSSION
The resonator-bolometer model provides a compact
description of KIBs in terms of the quality factors, sensitiv-
ities a and b, and thermal quantities C and G. The model of
(11) and the deﬁnitions of sensitivities in (4) are also applica-
ble to other types of resonator-bolometers as well, such as
dielectric bolometers 24 or AC biased transition edge sen-
sors.25 The bolometer model may also be used to design bol-
ometers in which electrothermal feedback enhances the
thermal response time. The model may be modiﬁed to
describe thermal couplings between the bolometer and other
thermal bodies by extending the bolometer matrix M or by
adding additional terms and differential equations to (14) or
(15), as is done in modeling other types of bolometers.
26,27
Additional noise vectors are required to describe noise asso-
ciated with additional couplings. Applications of such exten-
sions to the model include describing the response and noise
of weakly coupled absorbers and calculations of the effect of
thermal quasiparticle generation-recombination noise in the
bolometers.
The model may also be modiﬁed to describe MKIDs
which are not suspended on a membrane. In such a bolome-
ter model, C represents the electronic heat capacity of the
quasiparticles, the thermal quasiparticle recombination time
is given by C=G, and ﬂuctuations in the number of quasipar-
ticle Dn
qp are related to ﬂuctuations in the effective tempera-
ture TTB by Dnqp ¼ CTTB=Eqp . Additional noise sources and
coefﬁcients (such as optical efﬁciency go and bias power ef-
ﬁciency5 gA) may be included to represent athermal losses of
quasiparticles to the substrate.
