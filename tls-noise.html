IDC-based capacitor strategy for low electric field and low dielectric-loss noise.

Design Direction

Use an IDC (interdigitated capacitor) and prioritize low electric field in dielectric regions.

Because this application is not strongly area-limited, use larger capacitors and wider IDC gaps to reduce resonator voltage for a given stored energy, reducing TLS coupling.

TLS Noise Scaling

Use the standard fractional frequency-noise scaling model:

\[ \frac{S_f}{f_0^2}(\nu) \propto \frac{F\,\tan\delta_{TLS}}{\nu^{\beta}}. \]

Where:

  • \(F\): dielectric participation ratio
  • \(\tan\delta_{TLS}\): effective TLS loss tangent
  • \(\nu\): analysis (Fourier) frequency

IDC design intent: minimize \(F\) and local electric field magnitude \(|E|\) to suppress \(S_f/f_0^2\).

Citation: Gao et al. TLS model paper (arXiv:0804.0467).

For a worked first-pass numeric scaling estimate, see TLS Estimate.

Why Larger Capacitance Helps

For stored resonator energy \(U\):

\[ U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad V = \sqrt{\frac{2U}{C}}. \]

So increasing \(C\) lowers \(V\) and typical capacitor electric fields for the same readout energy, which reduces TLS drive and noise upconversion risk.

Does Lower Resonator Frequency Help TLS?

Yes, often indirectly. For fixed inductance \(L\), lowering \(f_0\) usually requires larger \(C\), and larger \(C\) reduces capacitor voltage and electric-field strength for the same stored energy.

\[ f_0=\frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}},\qquad V\propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{C}}. \]

That lower field can reduce TLS coupling in dielectrics, especially for IDC implementations with large gaps.

Important: lower \(f_0\) is not automatically lower TLS noise by itself; geometry, dielectric participation \(F\), and field distribution remain the dominant knobs.

Johnson Noise Formulas

Thermal (Johnson-Nyquist) voltage-noise PSD of resistance \(R\):

\[ S_V = 4k_BTR\quad[\mathrm{V^2/Hz}], \qquad e_n = \sqrt{4k_BTR}\quad[\mathrm{V/\sqrt{Hz}}]. \]

Equivalent current-noise PSD:

\[ S_I = \frac{4k_BT}{R}\quad[\mathrm{A^2/Hz}], \qquad i_n = \sqrt{\frac{4k_BT}{R}}\quad[\mathrm{A/\sqrt{Hz}}]. \]

These are the base formulas to propagate Johnson noise into resonator amplitude/phase (or \(I/Q\)) using your readout transfer matrix.

Johnson Noise in Phase (Resonator-Bolometer Form)

For phase readout, a common linearized form is to propagate the fundamental Johnson source through the phase responsivity:

\[ S_{\theta,J}(\nu)\approx \left|\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial X}\right|^2 S_{X,J}(\nu), \]

where \(X\) is the state variable used in your model (for example temperature, resistance, or dissipation/frequency state).

Equivalent transfer form from your \(I/Q\) model:

\[ S_{\theta,J}(\nu)\approx \mathbf{g}_{\theta}^{\dagger}(\nu)\,\mathbf{S}_{IQ,J}(\nu)\,\mathbf{g}_{\theta}(\nu), \]

with \(\mathbf{g}_{\theta}\) the local projection from \((I,Q)\) fluctuations to phase.

Project criterion: in-band TLS phase noise should not be significantly larger than Johnson-limited phase noise, i.e. \(S_{\theta,TLS}\lesssim S_{\theta,J}\) to stay near thermal/readout-limited performance.

Practical IDC Guidance for This Project

  • Prefer large-footprint IDC geometry to reduce field hot-spots.
  • Avoid deposited dielectrics where possible; favor vacuum/air participation.
  • Use symmetric layout and guard spacing to reduce parasitic field concentration.
  • Verify TLS floor in-band (around 100-200 kHz analysis frequencies).

Citation for geometry-driven TLS improvements: Gao et al. (arXiv:0804.0467).
